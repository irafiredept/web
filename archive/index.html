Open burning in Ira

By Vermont state law (10 V.S.A. § 2646), any open burning of natural wood or brush on your property requires a written permit from the town Fire Warden. Permits are free. We issue them day-of based on current conditions.

01

What you can burn

Natural wood, brush, leaves, and untreated lumber grown or cut on your own property. Material must be dry and piled no larger than 4 ft × 4 ft × 4 ft.

02

What is never allowed

Household trash, treated wood, tires, plastics, construction debris, or any material hauled in from off-site. No exceptions.

03

When you can burn

Permits are valid between dawn and dusk on the day issued. Night burning is not authorized. Permits are not issued when the Fire Weather Index reaches Very High or Extreme.

04

Your responsibilities

A responsible adult must be present for the duration of the burn, with a charged hose or 20 gallons of water and a shovel on hand. The fire must be fully extinguished before you leave the site.

Common questions

How do I request a permit?

Call the Fire Warden between 8:00 a.m. and 4:00 p.m. the day you intend to burn. Be ready to describe the location, size of the pile, and what you plan to burn. The Warden will check current conditions and either issue a permit verbally with a written copy to follow, or ask you to wait for a better day.

Is there a fee?

No. Permits are issued free of charge. Consider a donation to the department if you'd like to support our work.

Do I need a permit for a recreational campfire?

Not for a small, contained campfire (under 3 ft across) for warmth or cooking, provided it is in a fire ring, pit, or commercial fireplace. All other conditions — sober attendance, water on hand, fully extinguished — still apply.

What if my permit is suspended after it's issued?

If the Warden calls a suspension, extinguish your fire immediately and do not relight it. Your permit will be reissued the next permissible day at no cost. Suspensions are posted on this page and called out to active permit holders by phone.

I see smoke on my neighbor's property. Is that reportable?

If the fire appears unattended, is spreading, or is producing thick black or chemical-smelling smoke, call 9-1-1. If you're unsure whether a neighbor has a permit, you may call the non-emergency line during daytime hours.

Two ways to help your neighbors.

We're an all-volunteer department funded in part by the town and in large part by our neighbors. Give time or give money — either one keeps the trucks running and the crew trained.

Volunteer with us

No prior experience required. We train you — Firefighter I, EMR, and driver/operator certifications are all available at no cost. You need to be 18 (junior member at 16 with parental consent), live within a 10-minute drive of the station, and be able to commit to one drill night a month and calls as you're able.

Donate

A full set of structural turnout gear costs about $3,800. A new SCBA air pack is $7,200. An engine is a half-million dollars or more. Every gift — $20 or $2,000 — directly funds gear, training, and equipment for the people who answer your 9-1-1 call.
