# Product

## Register

brand

## Users

Residents of Ira (a real, small, rural, wooded town) and people in surrounding hollows. Many are 60+. They land on the site because they need to know something specific and time-sensitive: is today a burn day, what can I burn, is there an active alert, who do I call. A smaller share are residents considering volunteering or donating. The site is the department's public face on the open web; it is not an internal tool.

## Product Purpose

Give residents the information they need to act safely around fire, and reduce the volume of risky behavior that puts crews and neighbors at risk. Secondary purpose: recruit volunteers and accept donations from the same audience, without turning safety information into a marketing funnel. Success looks like a resident finding the burn rules, today's fire weather index, or the Fire Warden's number in under ten seconds, and trusting what they read.

## Brand Personality

Civic-utilitarian. Plain-spoken public information, closer in voice to a National Weather Service bulletin or a town-hall printed notice than to a marketing site. Steady, accurate, unfussy. Three words: **trustworthy, legible, of-the-place.** The site reads like it was written by people who know the woods, the roads, and the residents personally, because it was.

## Anti-references

- Generic SaaS / startup landing pages. No gradient mesh hero, no "Trusted by" logo wall, no purple-blue, no oversized rounded CTAs.
- Slick corporate firefighter branding. No glossy stock photos of crews silhouetted against sunsets, no "heroes" rhetoric, no recruitment-poster bravado.
- Trendy dark-mode tech aesthetic. No neon-on-black, no glassmorphism, no AI-workflow energy. This is a fire department, not a dev tool.
- Cheesy clipart civic web. No flame clipart, tiled backgrounds, marquee text, or "Welcome to our website" intros.

## Design Principles

1. **Information is the design.** The page is a public document. If the burn rules can't be scanned in ten seconds by a 65-year-old in bright sunlight on a phone, the design has failed, no matter how it looks.
2. **Of the place, not of the category.** Earth-tone palette and serif italic accents come from rural Ira, not from a "fire department" template. Decisions should feel local before they feel professional.
3. **Earned authority, not borrowed authority.** Trust is built by being accurate, current, and specific (today's index, the Warden's actual number), not by adopting government-website cosplay or corporate polish.
4. **Quiet warmth.** Civic-utilitarian doesn't mean cold. The neighborly register shows up in word choice and a few editorial italic accents, not in friendly stock photography or playful illustration.
5. **No festival of features.** Every section earns its place by serving a real resident task. When in doubt, cut.

## Accessibility & Inclusion

WCAG 2.2 AA, with explicit reduced-motion handling and keyboard-first navigation. Older-audience considerations apply throughout: minimum 17px body type with comfortable line-height, strong contrast (well above AA where practical), generous touch targets, no hover-only interactions, no motion that conveys information. Alert and weather-index components must be readable without color alone.
